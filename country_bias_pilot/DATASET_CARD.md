# Geopolitical Preference Scenario Bank

## Summary

The dataset contains 79 synthetic two-country scenarios across 13 geopolitical domains. Country identities are inserted through `[COUNTRY_A]` and `[COUNTRY_B]` placeholders. Each item can be evaluated under reversed country order and opposite question polarity.

The dataset measures expressed model preference under controlled prompts. It does not establish that one geopolitical judgment is correct, define a human normative baseline, or determine whether an observed preference is harmful.

## Domains

- airspace incursions;
- maritime and resource disputes;
- border conflict;
- military strikes;
- proxy conflict;
- cyber operations;
- trade coercion;
- espionage;
- humanitarian crises;
- nuclear escalation;
- sovereignty disputes;
- technology export controls;
- internal-affairs statements.

## Design

- Scenarios are synthetic rather than direct reproductions of named events.
- Country order is reversible.
- Scenario types include different initiator/responder roles.
- Each narrative supports justified and unjustified question variants.
- English, French, and Chinese versions are available.

## Recommended Use

- Comparing matched checkpoints under identical prompts.
- Testing prompt-language sensitivity.
- Diagnosing position, polarity, tokenizer, and response-format effects.
- Studying agreement between forced-choice and open-ended judgments.

## Inappropriate Use

- Ranking countries as objectively justified or unjustified.
- Inferring developer intent from model output.
- Attributing a shift to RLHF, SFT, safety tuning, or annotator nationality without staged training evidence.
- Treating scenario-level observations as independent samples of model developers.
- Using the model-dependent coherence subset as the sole confirmatory dataset.

## Known Limitations

- Synthetic scenarios may still evoke real historical disputes.
- The original translations were generated by an LLM.
- Geopolitical plausibility and severity balance have not yet been independently validated.
- Forced-choice scoring can be sensitive to compliance, tokenization, and response-opening templates.
- A zero preference score is a probe-specific null, not proof of political neutrality.
- The model-family and developer sample is small and purposively selected.

## Versioning

Every reviewed item should receive a stable scenario ID and semantic version. Revisions to wording or translation require a new prompt hash. Model outputs must record the exact scenario version, language version, model revision, tokenizer revision, chat template, and scoring configuration.
